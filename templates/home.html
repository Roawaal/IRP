Brexit   officially took place at 23:00 GMT on 31 January 2020 (00:00 1 February 2020 CET), marked the end of the UK’s longstanding 47-year alliance with the European Union. Now, after 4 years, British people are coming to realize the effects of Brexit on their own lives and livelihoods.

Agriculture holds a significant economic position in the UK, providing for the agri-food sector and participating in direct sales. The impacts of Brexit are expected to profoundly affect the role of agriculture in the UK, given that the EU's Common Agricultural Policy has shaped UK farming policies since the 1970s. Undoubtedly, these years has been a difficult period for these farmers, who have suffered with rising costs across multiple fronts, including higher fuel and energy expenses, as well as increased prices for fertilizers and animal feed. These financial pressures highlight the urgent need for greater support and recognition from political and institutional stakeholders.

This project aims to explore Brexit's wide-ranging effects on agricultral using diverse data analysis tools and techniques, including Google Trends analysis, regression analysis, and time series data analysis to review and forecast agricultural factors such as agriculture’s Gross Value Added (GVA), Gross Domestic Product (GDP), trade and employments.

UK's Overseas Trade

Since the UK has been a worldwide trading force, it is essential to evaluate the effects of Brexit on the UK’s overseas trade in agricultural stuff, excluding other goods like material manufactures or fuels. Annual changes of “Imports”, “Exports”, and “Trade Balance” values are preferred for evaluation rather than quarterly’s, as the seasonal fluctuations in UK agriculture are significant

Moreover, it will pay more attention to annual changes rather than quarterly's, as the seasonal fluctuations in UK agricultural are significant. For example, during the summer months, the UK can grow plenty of its own produce like lettuces and soft berries such as raspberries and strawberries, but when the weather turns colder Britain is forced to rely much more on imports from the EU.

Farm Subsidies Post-Brexit

CAP (EU's Common Agricultural Policy) is a system of agricultural subsidies and programmes covering farming, environmental measures and rural development. Having left the EU, the UK is now free to design its own agricultural policy to replace the CAP, although key elements of the CAP-based system currently remain in place.

Before Brexit, the government promised to match EU funding.11
Boris Johnson’s 2019 election manifesto had promised that post-Brexit replacement funding would “at a minimum match the size” of EU funds in “each nation” of the UK.

However, the critical question remains: Were these promises fulfilled?

The CAP is structured around two main components:
1) Pillar 1: including direct support and market support
2) Pillar 2: focusing on rural development, which includes the European Agricultural Fund for Rural Development (EAFRD) and Co-financing.

It indicates a shift in total agricultural payments following Brexit, suggesting the reconstruction in funding, correlating with the UK’s departure from the EU structures. The UK received around €4 billion in CAP funds each year before Brexit and nearly 80% of CAP funds is spent on Direct Payments to farmers under Pillar 1. It shows that the Direct Payments (Pillar 1) are the main system for providing income support to farm households within the CAP, but it ended in 2020 and legacy schemes have switched to UK funding.

UK's Labour Market

Brexit means the UK leaves the EU’s market and free movement principles, making it more difficult for workers from other EU countries to enter the UK to work. Before this, citizens of EU countries could freely enter the UK to work and live individually, but after Brexit, these workers met stricter visa requirements. It may seem surprising that the agri-food sector’s increased dependence on migrant labour has occurred at a time when many countries in the global North are trying to curb immigration levels. However, as noted, 'even politicians who oppose immigration often make an exception for seasonal farm workers'. While migrant labour has been a focal point in discussions about the post-Brexit agri-food system, the same level of attention has not been given to the migrant labourers themselves.

Brexit or no Brexit: Public opinions

Farmer Opinion

Farmers Weekly surveyed in 2016 asking 577 farmers how they were going to vote on Brexit and the farming vote outcome was 58% Leave, 31% Remain and 11% didn’t vote. Surprisingly, it is opposite to Country Land & Business Association (CLA)’s expectations because they expected the farming community would be overwhelmingly in favour of staying in the EU.

Farmers who intended to vote ”remain” expressed concerns about the future of farm subsidies and access to markets, both of which could significantly affect their profits if the UK were to leave the EU. The below figure lists the several factors highlighted by the remaining voters at the farm level.

Seven years on from the 2016 survey, Farmers Weekly investigated farmers’ opinions again indicating that 8% of them are willing to transfer from “leave” to “remain”if they could vote again. Moreover,the vast majority (83%) said Brexit has been worse than expected because it is not a good idea to cut ties with the largest free-trade area that’s right next to the UK.
