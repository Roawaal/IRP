Brexit  officially took place at 23:00 GMT on 31 January 2020 (00:00 1 February 2020 CET), marked the end of the UK’s longstanding 47-year alliance with the European Union.1 Now, after 4 years, British people are coming to realize the effects of Brexit on their own lives and livelihoods.

Agriculture holds a significant economic position in the UK, providing for the agri-food sector and participating in direct sales. The impacts of Brexit are expected to profoundly affect the role of agriculture in the UK, given that the EU's Common Agricultural Policy has shaped UK farming policies since the 1970s.

Exports to EU vs. Exports to Non-EU:
Both regions experienced a decline and saw a rebound from 2019-2020, 2021-2022 respectively. Exports to the EU dropped significantly (-14.76%), while exports to non-EU countries turned positive (+8.90%) from 2021 to 2020. From 2019 to 2022, over four years, exports to non-EU countries showed a growth (+10.89%), while exports to the EU decreased (-11.98%), suggesting a possible diversification of the UK's export markets or a shift in trade patterns post-Brexit.

Imports from EU vs. Imports from Non-EU:
Annual change rates for both of them have been opposite over the past four years and when looking at the change from 2019 to 2022, imports from non-EU countries increased (+6.89%), while imports from the EU decreased (-3.66%). It indicates a possiblely new balance of trade relations to new trade regulations after Brexit.
